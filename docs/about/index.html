about this site

You can’t doodle wrong.

Doodle Drills is a free, opinionated practice curriculum that takes someone who has never drawn before and walks them — drill by drill — to a level you could call professional sketchnoting.

Who it’s for

You. If you ever said “I can’t draw,” you’re the audience. If you’re an experienced sketchnoter, the early tiers will feel like warm-ups — skip to the tiers you need.

The bargain

Fifteen minutes a day, on paper, with a pen. The site keeps your place across visits. There’s no account; progress lives in your browser. Forty drills total, in seven tiers, roughly fifty hours.

Philosophy

Doodling is a visual language, not a talent. Like any language, you learn it through muscle memory, vocabulary, grammar, and reps. We optimize for legibility and ideas, not realism. We do not erase. We date the page. We move on.

A short FAQ

Do I need a tablet or fancy pen?

No. A 0.5mm fineliner (Sakura Micron 03, Staedtler Pigment Liner) and a plain dot-grid notebook will take you through Tier 6. Pick up a brush pen later if you want.

Can I jump ahead?

Yes. Locked tiers are a soft suggestion — you can peek at any drill any time. The locks just mean prior tiers built muscles you’ll lean on.

My drawings look bad. Am I doing it wrong?

No. Bad drawings are a normal part of the curve. Every drill says how it’ll feel when it clicks; that’s the only quality bar. Ugly pages stay in the notebook.

How do I move my progress to another device?

On the progress page, hit Export JSON. On the new device, hit Import JSON. There is no cloud sync.

Standing on shoulders

The curriculum is original but synthesized from teachers who built this field:

  • Mike Rohde — The Sketchnote Handbook (2012). The visual alphabet, the star person, the layout patterns.
  • Sunni Brown — The Doodle Revolution (2014).
  • Dan Roam — The Back of the Napkin (2008). The three primitives.
  • Betty Edwards — Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain (1979). Bypassing the judging mind with mechanical drills.
  • Lynda Barry — Syllabus (2014). The daily diary, the prescription against erasure.
  • Eva-Lotta Lamm, Doug Neill, Makayla Lewis — for showing what a personal style can look like.
